This paper revisits the embedding asymmetry between argument honorification and addressee honorification, with a focus on Korean <i>si</i> and supni. Korean argument honorific marker <i>si</i> is embeddable while the addressee honorific marker <i>supni</i> is not. First, this paper presents novel Korean data demonstrating that embedded si, typically classified as a content-oriented argument honorific marker, exhibits sensitivity to utterance context, including the presence of a direct addressee and the relative ranking of the speaker and addressee. I dub this innovative use of <i>si</i> as non-canonical use of si. This paper discusses how this non-canonical use of <i>si</i> reacts to the association between utterance sensitivity and embeddability proposed in Portner et al. (2019). At first glance, the novel Korean data on <i>si</i> appears to challenge Portner et al. (2019)’s generalization that utterance-oriented markers are not embeddable. However, I show how this non-canonical <i>si</i> can be reduced to its canonical counterpart, preserving the broader theoretical distinction between content-oriented and utterance-oriented markers. Syntactically, I argue that the non-canonical <i>si</i> retains the downward agreement dependency characteristic of its canonical counterpart. I propose that the observed utterance sensitivity arises from long-distance binding between the matrix addressee and a goal beyond the subject position. While <i>si</i> typically agrees with the subject in its canonical use, I argue that in its innovative use, <i>si</i> can target a broader set of discourse-salient event participants, such as affectees/beneficiaries. This approach captures the indirect addressee honorification effect while maintaining <i>si</i>’s core status as an argument honorific marker.
