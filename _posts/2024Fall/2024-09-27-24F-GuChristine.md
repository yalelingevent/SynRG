In the grammatical tradition of Tibetan, the ergative case (བྱེད་སྒྲ་, byed sgra) has been described as obligatorily present only when the subject receives pragmatic emphasis and optional in discourse-neutral contexts (McGregor 2010; DeLancey 1984, 2011, among others). This presents a challenge to theories of ergative case assignment, which view the ERG case as either inherent to its 𝜃-role (Legate 2006, 2008) or dependent on the configurational relationship between two nominals (Marantz 1991; Baker 2014). In this talk, I present novel fieldwork data on adverb ordering, nominalization, and prosody, which support a movement-based analysis of case optionality. Specifically, I adopt an AGREE-based dependent case framework in the style of Poole (2024) that allows ERG and ABS to be assigned non-simultaneously in a clause bifurcated into two assigning domains. With the Voice head mediating dependent ERG, this analysis also predicts case alternations in raising-to-ergative constructions and non-canonical word orders.
